Statistical Analysis

To understand how to get better at Figgie, we must first understand the odds. The card distribution is the foundation ‚Äî everything flows downstream from knowing how likely a given hand composition is, and what it implies about the hidden state of the deck.

‚ô†
Deck Structure

40 cards across 4 suits. One suit has 12 cards (common), another 10, and the remaining two 8 each. The common suit's cross-suit is the target ‚Äî worth $10 per card at end of round.

‚ô•
Target Suit

The target suit is cross-suits with the common suit: ‚ô†‚Üî‚ô£ and ‚ô•‚Üî‚ô¶. If ‚ô† has 12, ‚ô£ is the target. The pot is $200 ‚Äî split by who holds target cards at end of trading.

‚ô¶
Hidden Information

Neither the common suit nor the target is revealed. Players must infer them from hand composition, market behavior, and sequential Bayesian updating.

‚ô£
Information Edge

Superior inference about the target suit ‚Äî or about what others believe ‚Äî is the core alpha. This is a game about epistemics as much as trading.

Bayesian Inference

Starting Hand Analysis

The most important suit in the game is the target suit ‚Äî the only one that gets scored at end of round. To find it, we need to identify the common suit, which always holds exactly 12 cards. Since the common suit has the most cards, there is an implicit prior that any given suit in our hand is more likely to be common the more of it we hold.

If we're dealt 6 hearts, 2 spades, 1 diamond, and 1 club ‚Äî how confident should we be that hearts is the common suit? More precisely:

Using Bayes' theorem with uniform prior over 4 suits:
P(common = S | n_S) ‚àù P(n_S | common = S) ¬∑ P(common = S)

Multi-Suit Posterior

We shouldn't limit our inference to the most-held suit alone. Every card in the hand is a signal. Holding 7 spades and 2 hearts jointly updates our posterior across all four suit hypotheses simultaneously.

Expected Value & Rational Pricing

Given a posterior distribution over which suit is the target, we can derive rational bid/ask prices for each card. A card of suit S should trade at roughly its expected end-of-round value ‚Äî the $10 payout weighted by the probability that S is indeed the target.
